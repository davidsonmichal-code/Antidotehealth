Data Quality Failures

  • Records that exist but are wrong
  • Non-active credentialing status in live records
  • Expired accreditation served to member search
  • Missing location data, stale office hours
  • Fix owner: PNMS ops team

Coverage Gaps

  • Services that don't exist in the network at all
  • High-demand specialties with too few providers
  • Geographic deserts with no nearby providers
  • No visibility into which gaps cause member harm
  • Fix owner: network adequacy + business dev

Four Users, Four Different Problems

Map users before designing anything
UserCore NeedPain Today
Member Find an available, in-network provider Search returns stale or wrong data - wasted appointments, wrong cost expectations
Claims Team Verify provider is active and contracted at time of service Manual lookups, wrong denials, adjudication delays - most expensive failure
Credentialing Team Track license and contract expirations proactively No alerts system - reactive fixes after expiration, fully preventable
Compliance Audit every change to a provider record No audit trail, no timestamp, no attribution - regulatory liability
Key insight: Claims denial is the most expensive failure. Credentialing expiration is the most preventable. Compliance audit trail is non-negotiable - you cannot modify credentialing records without attribution.

Master Provider Record - Five Core Components

One principle: make the record trustworthy before building anything on top
  • 1
    Multi-partner ingestion pipeline Normalizes partner feeds from different formats into a canonical schema. Schema drift triggers an alert - it does not silently drop fields.
  • 2
    Master Provider Record (NPI-keyed) Single source of truth, deduplicated by NPI - the federal ID every provider has. Partners may use different internal IDs for the same doctor; NPI is the correct key.
  • 3
    Credentialing validation and status workflow Automated checks against NPPES, CAQH, state license boards + human review queue for exceptions.
  • 4
    Exception queue Records that fail validation go here - not into the live record. You never merge a bad record and then try to fix it after.
  • 5
    Claims eligibility and provider search APIs The downstream outputs. Claims needs active + contracted status. Member search needs availability + location.

MVP - Make the Record Trustworthy

Scope discipline: nothing in Phase 2 delivers value if the data is wrong
  • 1
    Master Provider Record (NPI-keyed deduplication)
  • 2
    Multi-partner ingestion and normalization pipeline
  • 3
    Provider credentialing validation and status workflow
  • 4
    External data source verification (NPPES, CAQH, license boards)
  • 5
    Data freshness monitoring and expiration alerting
  • 6
    Provider record audit log and change history
  • 7
    Exception queue and manual remediation workflow
  • 8
    Claims eligibility and provider search APIs
  • 9
    Operational data quality and SLA metrics

Phase Gate - Required before Phase 2

  • 95% record completeness
  • Zero same-day SLA misses for 2 consecutive weeks
  • Claims denial rate attributable to provider data trending down
Why this is the right boundary: you cannot build coverage intelligence on untrustworthy data. If 25% of records have wrong credentialing status, your gap analysis will undercount active providers and over-flag gaps that don't exist.

Phase 2 - Intelligence on Trusted Data

Every Phase 2 feature has a dependency on the MVP phase gate
  • 1
    Network coverage intelligence Automated gap detection by geography and specialty. Depends on: trustworthy active-status records.
  • 2
    Provider search relevance optimization Rank by profile completeness + availability. Depends on: complete profiles to rank meaningfully.
  • 3
    Referral routing recommendations Route members to best available match. Depends on: reliable location + specialty data.
  • 4
    Provider self-service updates Providers update their own records with verification. Depends on: verified verification layer.
  • 5
    Advanced data quality analytics and trend reporting Rich analytics on top of MVP telemetry. Depends on: baseline operational metrics.

Why these are NOT in MVP

Coverage gap analysis on untrustworthy data produces misleading results - you will over-flag gaps and under-flag real ones.

Search relevance optimization requires complete profiles to rank meaningfully. Ranking incomplete data creates worse results than no ranking.

Provider self-service requires trust in the verification layer before letting providers write directly to production records.

Zero-Result Classification - Why It Matters

Same symptom. Three different owners. Three different fixes.
The problem: A zero-result search looks identical in raw data whether caused by a geographic gap, a missing specialty, or a stale record. If you just count zero results without classifying them, you cannot route the problem.

How You Know the System is Working

Four dimensions - they move at different speeds

Data Quality (leading - daily)

  • Record completeness rate (target: 95%+)
  • Same-day SLA compliance (target: 100%)
  • Exception queue resolution time
  • Partner ingestion error rate

Member Outcomes (lagging - days/weeks)

  • Search success rate
  • Search abandonment rate
  • Telehealth substitution rate*
  • Repeated unsuccessful search cohort

Operational Efficiency

  • Credentialing cycle time
  • Exception queue avg resolution time
  • Partner feed latency

Business Impact (longest lag - weeks/months)

  • Claims denial rate attributable to provider data errors
  • Member NPS for search experience
*Telehealth substitution rate: members who got zero in-person results AND then searched for/booked virtual care for the same specialty. Genuine substitution, not preference. High rate = in-person network gap that telehealth is covering.

Q&A Prep

Product • Data • Architecture • Dev • UI

Product Questions

Prioritization, scoping, build vs. buy, success metrics
Why did you scope MVP the way you did?
The principle is: don't build intelligence on top of unreliable data. If 25% of records have wrong credentialing status, a coverage gap analysis will undercount active providers and mislead you. The phase gate is measurable - 95% completeness, zero SLA misses for two weeks, denials trending down. Nothing in Phase 2 unlocks until those three conditions are true.
Build vs. buy?
Buy what already exists: NPPES and CAQH give you federal verification - no reason to replicate that. Build what is differentiating: the master record layer, delta detection against partner feeds, and coverage intelligence logic specific to Antidote's virtual-first model. That's where the IP lives. You don't build a database; you build the layer that makes the data usable.
How would you prioritize within MVP?
By severity of downstream impact. Sanctions and expired credentials are same-day SLA - a wrong provider state can cause a wrongful claim. Location data is 72-hour SLA. Office hours are 7-day SLA because they're operationally important but not compliance-critical. The exception queue is MVP because you need a human review step before you can trust the automation.
What would you do differently with 6 months instead of 6 weeks?
6 weeks: make the master record trustworthy for highest-severity fields. 6 months: coverage intelligence, provider self-service portal, search relevance ranking. Also: provider response rate monitoring - the weakest link is providers ignoring update requests. Tracking that and escalating through partner relationships is what makes the system self-maintaining.

Data Questions

Deduplication, classification, metrics definitions
What is zero-result classification and why does it matter?
A zero-result search looks identical in raw data whether caused by a geographic gap, a missing specialty, or a stale record. Each has a different owner and different fix timeline - 24 hours vs. months. Without classification you route noise and the ops team ignores alerts. The classification logic is what makes the system actionable.
How does deduplication work when two records conflict?
NPI is the primary key. When two partner feeds send conflicting data for the same NPI, a conflict record goes to the exception queue. The live record is not updated until a human reviewer resolves it. Source hierarchy is defined upfront: NPPES overrides partner feeds for regulatory fields like credentialing status and sanctions. You never overwrite a live record with an unresolved conflict.
What is telehealth substitution rate and why is the definition important?
Members who got zero in-person results and then searched for or booked a virtual visit for the same specialty. It specifically measures genuine substitution - not members who preferred telehealth from the start. A high rate in a specific specialty tells you the in-person network has a gap telehealth is covering. A low rate means members are not accepting virtual as an alternative - which means the gap is causing real unmet demand.
What are the data dependencies for zero-result classification?
Three data sources must be joined: (1) search event log (the zero-result query + location + specialty), (2) provider network coverage data (which specialties are contracted in which geographies), (3) PNMS record state (which providers exist but have stale/expired records). A zero result is a data quality failure only if a matching provider exists in PNMS but was excluded from search. Without joining all three you cannot classify.

Architecture Questions

System design, consistency, scale, compliance
Walk me through the data flow.
Partner feeds arrive in different formats. Ingestion normalizes to a canonical schema and deduplicates by NPI. Validation layer checks mandatory fields and cross-references NPPES/CAQH. Records that pass go to the Master Provider Record. Records that fail go to the exception queue for human review - never into the live record. The MPR serves two downstream consumers: the claims eligibility API and the member search API. Every step writes to the audit log.
How do you handle eventual consistency between the master record and search?
Search is a read replica. The SLA on propagation is defined in the serving layer contract. For credentialing status changes - same-day propagation, because a wrong status can cause a wrongful claim. For office hours - 7-day propagation is acceptable because it's operationally important but not compliance-critical. The SLA tiers are different by field type, not uniform across all fields.
What are the security and compliance requirements?
Three areas: HIPAA (provider records may contain PHI, protected in transit and at rest), RBAC (claims, credentialing, and compliance teams have different permissions - a claims processor cannot modify a credentialing record), and audit logging (every change is attributable, timestamped, and queryable for regulatory review). Retention: even after a provider leaves the network, their historical record must be accessible for claims adjudication audits. You cannot delete records referenced by a past claim.
What is not automated yet in MVP?
Conflict resolution is manual in MVP - when two sources disagree on a field, a human resolves it. Coverage gap detection is manual - the dashboard shows zero-result patterns but does not automatically trigger network adequacy actions. Provider outreach is manual - when a record is stale, ops emails or calls the provider. These become automated in Phase 2 once the manual process is understood well enough to encode rules.

Dev Questions

Pipeline design, schema management, API contracts
How does the ingestion pipeline handle schema drift from partners?
The normalization layer maps each partner schema to the canonical schema. Schema drift - a new field or renamed field in a partner feed - triggers an alert and goes to a schema validation queue. It does not silently drop fields into the record. The mapping is partner-versioned so you can run two versions in parallel during partner format transitions. Breaking changes require a new mapping version, not an in-place edit.
What does the API contract look like for claims eligibility?
Request: NPI + date of service. Response: active status (boolean), contracted status (boolean), credentialing valid through (date), last verified timestamp. The response must include the last-verified timestamp so claims systems can flag cases where the verification is older than the SLA window. The API does not return the full provider record - only the fields needed for eligibility decisions. Separation of concerns.
How would you scale the validation queue if partner volumes spike?
The external verification calls (NPPES, CAQH) are the bottleneck - they are synchronous API calls with rate limits. In MVP I'd use a queue with backpressure so spikes don't drop records. For Phase 2, I'd cache verification results with a TTL matching the SLA window - credentialing status doesn't change minute to minute, so you don't need to re-verify every ingestion event. Cache invalidation triggers on expiration date approach or manual override.
How do you test the validation logic without breaking production?
Shadow mode: run the new validation rules against incoming records but don't act on failures - just log them. Compare the shadow failure rate against the production exception queue rate for 2-3 days before enabling. This catches cases where a new rule flags records that the current system accepts - which might be correct (we're tightening rules) or a false positive (the rule has a bug). Shadow mode is how you build confidence before gating real records.

UI / UX Questions

Dashboard design, filter logic, usability measurement
Why is the Insights Panel the primary surface instead of charts?
Because the user is an ops lead reviewing the dashboard under time pressure. If they have to interpret five charts to figure out what's wrong, that's a design failure. The three-card insights panel does the classification work for them: root cause, specific finding, owner, drill-down link. Charts are investigation tools - you use them after the panel tells you what to look at. A chart is only useful once you already know what you're looking for.
How do you decide what goes in the Insights Panel vs. the charts?
The panel surfaces actionable issues - things that require an owner to do something this week. Charts surface patterns - things that inform strategy or investigation. The threshold for the panel is: does this require a specific person to act within a defined SLA window? If yes, panel. If it's a trend worth understanding but no immediate action required, chart. The alert banner at the top of the dashboard is even more urgent - active issues with SLA breach risk.
How would you test if the dashboard is actually useful?
Measure whether the ops team acts on alerts within the target SLA window before and after launch. If alert volume goes up but action rate stays flat, the alerts are noise. Also track how often the team drills down from the insights panel vs. going straight to the charts - high chart-first usage means the panel isn't doing its job. Finally: ask ops leads to rate their confidence in knowing "what is broken and why" after each session.
Why did you make all filters composable instead of radio buttons?
Because real ops questions are multi-dimensional: "show me zero-result rates for Cardiology members on the Basic plan in the Southwest." A radio button forces you to pick one dimension. Composable filters let you answer the actual diagnostic questions. The design challenge is communicating when multiple filters are active - I solved this with the active filter indicator bar and a clear-all button, so the user always knows what subset they're looking at.

Hard Questions - What They're Really Testing

Pushback, tradeoffs, and things that could have gone differently
Isn't the exception queue just a workaround for bad validation rules?
Yes, partially - and that's intentional. You can't automate what you don't understand yet. The exception queue generates the data that lets you improve the validation rules. Every record that goes through human review teaches you what the rule missed. The queue is MVP because it's the learning mechanism. Removing it to "force automation" means bad records either get rejected silently or pass into production. Neither is better.
Claims APIs in MVP seems like scope creep - the system isn't ready yet.
The APIs are what give the system value. A trustworthy master record with no downstream consumer is a data warehouse project. The claims team is the primary business justification for MVP. If the APIs aren't in MVP, you can't demonstrate that improved data quality reduces denials - which is the phase gate metric. The APIs are what make the phase gate measurable.
Why not start with the coverage intelligence layer - that's where the member value is?
Coverage intelligence on bad data is worse than no intelligence. If 25% of records have wrong credentialing status, your gap analysis will flag gaps in areas where providers exist but their records are stale. You'll send network adequacy teams to contract providers in markets where you already have coverage. That's not just wasted effort - it's misleading the business. Trustworthy data is not a prerequisite detail; it's the foundation the value sits on.
